A useful AI prompt gives the tool five things: the task, relevant context, constraints, the format you want back, and a way to check the result. Longer is not automatically better. Specific and testable is better.

Most weak prompts miss one of those five. The fix is not a secret wording. It is giving the tool enough to work with, then editing the answer the way you would edit a rough draft from a person.

If you are completely new to what these tools can and cannot do, the [AI for beginners guide](/articles/ai-for-beginners) covers the basics first. This article assumes you have opened a chat tool once or twice and want more reliable results.

## The five-part formula

Use this structure for almost any request. You do not need all five every time, but checking each one prevents the most common failures.

1. **Task** - one clear sentence stating what you want. "Summarize this article in five bullet points" beats "help with this."
2. **Context** - who it is for, what they already know, what came before. "For a small-business owner who has never used a CRM" tells the tool what level to write at.
3. **Constraints** - length, tone, things to avoid. "Under 150 words, friendly, no jargon, do not use exclamation marks."
4. **Format** - how you want the output structured. "A numbered list," "a two-column comparison table," "three short paragraphs."
5. **Check** - a way to verify the result is right. "End with one sentence on what I should confirm against the source."

A single reusable example makes this concrete. Suppose you want help drafting a polite follow-up to a client who has not replied.

> "Write a follow-up email to a client who has not responded to my proposal sent eight days ago. The client is a dental office manager, not technical. Keep it under 120 words, warm but professional, and do not use the word 'urgent.' Give me the email, then list two assumptions you made that I should verify before sending."

That prompt has all five parts. The last line is the check. It asks the tool to surface its own guesses so you can catch them.

## A weak prompt versus an improved one

The gap between a weak and improved prompt is usually context, not effort.

**Weak:** "Write a bio for me."

The tool has nothing to work with. It will produce a generic paragraph that sounds like everyone else's.

**Improved:** "Write a 100-word professional bio for a freelance graphic designer named Maya who specializes in packaging design for small food brands. Mention her five years of experience and that she works remotely from Austin. Tone is confident but approachable, not salesy. Return one paragraph I can paste into a LinkedIn About section."

The improved version takes 20 more seconds to write and returns something close to usable. You will still edit it, but you are editing substance, not rewriting from scratch.

## Prompts for common tasks

The formula adapts to whatever you are doing. Here are four everyday uses.

### Email

> "Rewrite this draft to sound warmer and 20 percent shorter, keeping the meeting time and the RSVP link. Do not add new information. Here is the draft: [paste]."

Tell it what to keep and what not to invent. Email prompts go wrong when the tool adds details you never approved.

### Research

> "I am comparing two project management tools for a three-person team. Summarize the main differences in pricing, free-tier limits, and learning curve as a table. Flag anything you are not certain about. Do not recommend one yet."

Research prompts need the check step most. AI can invent feature lists and prices. Ask it to mark uncertainty, then verify the deciding factors yourself before you act.

### Planning

> "Draft a realistic one-week plan for launching a simple email newsletter. I can spend about one hour per day. I already have a list of 80 contacts and a Mailchimp account. Give me day-by-day tasks and tell me which day is most likely to slip."

Planning prompts benefit from naming your constraints up front. The tool will not plan around limits it does not know about.

### Learning

> "Explain what a large language model is as if I have a marketing background but no coding experience. Use one analogy, keep it under 200 words, and end with one question I should ask to test whether I understood."

Learning prompts work best when you name your existing knowledge. The tool calibrates the explanation to what you already know.

## How to ask follow-up questions

The first answer is a draft, not a verdict. Follow-up questions are where the result gets useful.

Good follow-ups are specific. "Make it shorter" works. "Cut the second paragraph and move the pricing detail to the top" works better because it tells the tool exactly what to change.

If the answer is off-track, say why. "This sounds too formal for a text message" tells the tool more than "try again." If you do not know why it feels wrong, ask the tool to explain its own choice. "Why did you choose a bulleted list instead of a table?" often surfaces an assumption you can then correct.

A back-and-forth of three or four rounds usually beats one long prompt. You do not need to get it perfect on the first try.

## How to verify factual output

AI tools can state incorrect facts with full confidence. This is the single most important habit to build.

Treat any specific claim as unverified until you check it. That includes names, dates, prices, statistics, legal points, medical information, quotes, and anything with a source the tool cannot show you.

A practical verification flow:

1. Ask the tool for its sources. Some tools can cite where a claim came from. If it cannot, treat the claim as a starting point, not a fact.
2. Check the claim against the original source. A quick search for the exact statistic or quote usually takes less than a minute.
3. If the claim matters to a decision, verify it twice. One source can be wrong or out of date.

For anything that affects money, health, legal rights, or safety, a qualified human checks the work. The tool is a starting point, not the final word. [Module 2 covers what to do when AI gets it wrong](/modules/module-2/when-ai-gets-it-wrong) in more depth.

## Five mistakes beginners make

1. **Writing one huge prompt and hoping.** Long does not mean clear. If a prompt has ten instructions, the tool will miss some. Break big requests into steps.
2. **Copying prompts without adapting them.** A prompt that worked for someone else's task may not fit yours. Change the context and constraints to match your situation.
3. **Trusting confident tone.** A smooth, certain answer is not more likely to be correct. Verify the claims that matter.
4. **Giving up after the first answer.** The first response is usually the roughest. Editing through follow-ups is where the tool earns its keep.
5. **Pasting private information.** Remove real names, account numbers, passwords, and client details before you ask for help with real work. Use placeholders like "Customer A" while drafting.

## The habit to build

Prompting is a skill you build by noticing what changed the result. When an answer is surprisingly good, look back at what you asked. When it misses, figure out which of the five parts was vague or missing.

You do not need to memorize formulas. You need to practice giving the tool a clear task, real context, sensible limits, a desired shape, and a way to check the work. Do that a dozen times and the structure becomes automatic.

Start with one low-stakes task today. Use the five-part formula, run one follow-up, and verify one factual claim. That is enough to feel the difference between a prompt that works and one that wastes your time.
